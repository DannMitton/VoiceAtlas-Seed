# Reading Notes: Stark Ch. 5 — Vocal Tremulousness: The Pulse of Singing

<!-- p. 121–122 -->
**The Problem of Vocal Tremulousness:**
- Three distinct mechanisms of vocal tremulousness:
  1. **Vibrato** — continuous legato pitch undulation; neuromuscular in origin.
  2. **Glottal articulation** (*trillo*, laryngeal trill) — rapid opening and closing of arytenoid cartilages; quasi-staccato interruption of tone.
  3. **Respiratory impulse** — conscious use of respiratory muscles to rapidly raise and lower subglottal pressure.
- Glottal articulation is fastest; vibrato second; respiratory impulse slowest.
- Historical treatises rarely distinguish clearly between these three mechanisms.
- Seashore's definition of good vibrato: "a pulsation of pitch, usually accompanied with synchronous pulsations of loudness and timbre, of such extent and rate as to give a pleasing flexibility, tenderness, and richness to the tone" (Seashore 1932, 7).

<!-- p. 123–130 -->
**The Early Treatises:**
- Vicentino (1555) and Finck (1556): criticised coarse glottal articulation — compared to bleating goats and confused racket.
- Zacconi (1592): *tremolo* = "the true portal by which to enter into *passaggi* and the learning of *gorgie*"; described as narrow, attractive, continuous; linked to expressive purpose. Ambiguous — could be vibrato or glottal articulation.
- Conforto (c.1593): distinguished *trillo* (reiterated single note) from *groppo* (alternation of two adjacent tones).
- Bovicelli (1594): *tremolo* = "trembling of the voice over a single note"; implied same mechanism for *groppetto* and *tremolo* — physiologically questionable.
- Caccini (1602): *trillo* = restriking each note "with the throat" (*ribattere ciascuna nota con la gola*); taught by starting slowly and accelerating to 32nd notes. Claimed same technique for *trillo* and *gruppo* — Stark disputes this on physiological grounds: pitch reversal in *gruppo* likely requires a different, slower mechanism than glottal articulation.
- Mersenne (1636): appreciated tremulousness in Italian singing; used voice as model for instruments.
- Doni (c.1630s): distinguished *trillo* (cheerful, rapid ripple) from *tremolamento* (imperfect trillo, feminine quality, best avoided in sad or heroic subjects); suggested vocal vibrato imitated string vibrato rather than vice versa.
- Bacilly (1668): *cadence* (vibrato) a gift of nature; not all voices possessed it; could be refined through training; strong voices often had erratic cadence needing correction.
- Friderici (1614), Praetorius (1619), Herbst (1642): German authors described a "trembling, floating, pulsating" voice as a desirable quality — almost certainly vibrato, not glottal articulation.

> [Stark, pp. 123–130]

**Claim:** The early treatises describe at least two distinct forms of vocal tremulousness — glottal articulation (*trillo*) and what appears to be normal vibrato — but rarely distinguish between them clearly; Caccini's claim that both *trillo* and *gruppo* use the same mechanism is physiologically doubtful.

**Eighteenth-century Sources:**
- Johann Agricola (1757): vibrato (*Bebung*) on long notes; compared to string vibrato; "especially effective on long sustained notes, particularly when applied toward the end."
- Montéclair (1736) and Bérard (1755): vibrato should be narrow, almost imperceptible — no more than a quarter-tone in extent.
- Mattheson (1739): tremolo = "the slightest possible oscillation on a fixed tone"; distinguished from trillo (two adjacent notes) and goat-bleat; wrongly ascribed vibrato to action of the epiglottis.
- Tosi (1723): did not mention vibrato; described two kinds of *passaggi* — beaten (*battuto*) and gliding (*scivolato*); criticised *mordente fresco* (reiterated single tone) as laughable.
- Mancini (1774): vibrato (*vibrare*) listed as a necessary part of the cultivated voice alongside brio, agility, and expression (1777 edition).
- Mozart (1774 letter): described a bad vibrato as notes becoming "distinct crotchets or quavers" — a slow wobble; said the human voice "trembles naturally — but in its own way — and only to such a degree that the effect is beautiful."
- Leopold Mozart: vibrato (*tremolo*) "an ornament that originates in Nature herself."

<!-- p. 134–137 -->
**Nineteenth-century Sources:**
- Garcia: advocated "steadiness of sound, free from every sort of tremor or quavering" (*Hints on Singing*, 1894); allowed tremolo only for highly emotional passages, with "taste and moderation."
- Paradox: Garcia's insistence on firm glottal closure, lowered larynx, and elevated subglottal pressure creates the very muscular antagonisms that tend to produce vibrato; his own pupils (Marchesi lineage: Melba, Eames, Calvé) all sang with vibrato.
- Francesco Lamperti: distinguished tremulousness (vocal defect from strain) from "oscillation" (good effect of a strong, vibrating, sonorous voice).
- Rubini controversy: some authors attributed introduction of vibrato to the operatic stage to tenor Giovanni-Battista Rubini (career c.1830–45); Garcia reportedly blamed Rubini; Chorley and Klein both criticised the practice as spreading from Paris; Metfessel (1932) suggested Rubini used an exaggerated vibrato as an expressive embellishment rather than introducing it as a continuous feature.

> [Stark, pp. 134–135]

**Claim:** Garcia's apparent rejection of continuous vibrato is difficult to reconcile with his prescriptions for firm glottal closure and elevated subglottal pressure, which are precisely the muscular conditions modern science identifies as generating vibrato; his protégés all sang with vibrato.

**Modern Views of the Trillo:**
- Glottal articulation: rapid abduction/adduction of arytenoids; aided by aerodynamic forces and elastic recoil.
- Faster than vibrato: up to 12 pulses per second (vs. 5–8 for vibrato); not completely regular.
- Works well for reiterated single notes and unidirectional scalar passages; probably cannot execute pitch reversals (as in *gruppo*) due to slower pitch-changing mechanism.
- Most successful in singers using low subglottal pressures; better suited to high voices, falsettists, tenors.

<!-- p. 138–142 -->
**Modern Views of Vibrato (Seashore and Iowa Studies):**
- Normal vibrato rate: 5–8 pulsations per second; pitch oscillation 0.5–1.0 semitone (acceptable up to 1.5); synchronous intensity fluctuations of 2–3 dB.
- Bad vibrato: *tremolo* (too fast); *wobble* (rate ≤4.0, pitch fluctuation >1.5 tones).
- Vibrato rate has generally slowed since early 20th century: Caruso ~7.0 Hz; Pavarotti ~5.5 Hz; possibly due to cultural preference for darker voice quality associated with *chiaroscuro* and *appoggio*.
- Seashore: vibrato present in "all great artists in about 95% of their phonated time."
- Sundberg: "Almost all professional opera singers develop vibrato without thinking about it... vibrato develops more or less by itself as voice training proceeds successfully."

> [Stark, p. 146]

**Claim:** Vibrato is a natural periodicity of the vocal muscles under stress, present in virtually all trained voices; straight tone in a trained singer requires active inhibition of vibrato and implies muscle rigidity.
**Type:** Empirical Finding
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Vibrato

<!-- p. 138–140 -->
**Neuromuscular Origins of Vibrato:**
- Vibrato = manifestation of general neuromuscular tremor; work-rest cycle of muscles alternately contracting and relaxing to prevent fatigue.
- Schoen (1922): vibrato parallels tremor in every measurable parameter — constant rate, variable amplitude, involuntary, emerges under slight muscular strain, approximately half the rate of normal muscular discharge.
- Chief muscles: cricothyroid (principal pitch-regulating muscle) opposed by thyroarytenoids; Mason and Zemlin (1966): cricothyroid active in phase with pitch and intensity crests 82% of the time.

<!-- p. 142–144 -->
**Rate, Extent, and Respiratory Control:**
- Richard Miller: slower modern vibrato rate may reflect cultivation of darker voice quality (*chiaroscuro*, *appoggio*); muscular antagonisms of these techniques cause vibrato slowing.
- Respiratory control of vibrato: contested; Rothenberg: likely "a small respiratory-induced component"; Shipp, Sundberg, and Haglund (1985): singer has no conscious control over vibrato *rate* but can control extent.
- Vibrato and floridity: Vennard — florid passages "ride on the impulse of the vibrato"; Sundberg — singers "switch off" vibrato during florid passages; Miller — vibrato frequency need not coincide with note change rate. Question unresolved.

<!-- p. 144–149 -->
**Vibrato and Expressiveness:**
- Seashore: beauty in vibrato consists of enrichment of tone (timbre and sonance fusion), flexibility (relief from rigidity), and tenderness (instability as symbol of emotion).
- Vibrato mitigates acoustical beats between mistuned partials; allows slight expressive pitch deviation without sounding out of tune; Sundberg: "Singing without vibrato must be a very tricky thing indeed."
- Pitch perception: vibrato tone perceived at approximately the average fundamental frequency; vibrato does not reduce intonation accuracy (Sundberg).

<!-- p. 150–152 -->
**Stark's Conclusions:**
- Glottal articulation (*trillo*): clearly present in late 16th/early 17th century; quasi-staccato; fell out of fashion likely because except in skilled hands it was not musically appealing; still used in Monteverdi to Rossini repertoire.
- Normal vibrato: almost certainly always present in cultivated singing as a natural periodicity of muscles under stress; its absence from some treatises may reflect ubiquity rather than absence.
- Key synthesis: all the major techniques associated with classical singing — *coup de la glotte*, *voix sombrée*, *lutte vocale*, *appoggio*, *Stauprinzip*, *messa di voce* — require skilful contraction of opposing muscle groups, and will likely result in vibrato unless actively inhibited.
- Conclusion: "There has always been a plurality of styles of vocal tremulousness" — doctrinaire positions on historical authenticity are unwarranted.

> [Stark, pp. 151–152]

**Claim:** All the major classical vocal techniques — *appoggio*, *lutte vocale*, *coup de la glotte*, *voix sombrée*, *messa di voce* — involve muscular antagonisms that will naturally produce vibrato unless actively suppressed; vibrato is therefore not an add-on embellishment but an emergent property of the cultivated singing voice.
