# Reading Notes: Stark Ch. 7 — Bel Canto: Context and Controversy

---

## Reprise: Toward a Definition of Bel Canto (pp. 189–190)

> [Stark, p. 189]

**Claim:** Stark's formal definition of *bel canto* comprises two related but distinct dimensions: (1) a refined method of voice production in which glottal source, vocal tract, and respiratory system interact to create chiaroscuro, *appoggio*, register equalisation, malleability of pitch and intensity, and a pleasing vibrato, along with idiomatic use of onset, legato, portamento, glottal articulation, *messa di voce*, *mezza voce*, floridity, trills, and *tempo rubato*; (2) any musical style that employs this voice tastefully and expressively.
**Type:** Interpretive Synthesis
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Bel Canto

## The Establishment of the Old Italian School (pp. 190–197)

> [Stark, p. 190]

**Claim:** Stark traces the origins of the old Italian school to three sequential phases: the Venetian cantatrici and pseudo-monody of the 1550s–60s; the Ferrarese and Mantuan *concerto delle donne* from 1580 onward; and the Florentine "noble manner of singing" codified by Caccini in *Le nuove musiche* (1602).
**Type:** Historical Synthesis
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Bel Canto; Old Italian School

> [Stark, pp. 191–192, citing Newcomb 1980, 7–8]

**Claim:** Stark, following Newcomb, marks 1580 as the dividing line between amateur courtier-singers and professional virtuosi at the Ferrarese court; the *concerto delle donne* was made up of "singers who, because of their musical abilities, were made courtiers," not the reverse.
**Type:** Direct Observation
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Old Italian School

> [Stark, p. 193, citing Giustiniani, *Discorso sopra la musica*, ca. 1628]

**Claim:** Giustiniani's account of the Mantuan *concerto delle donne* documents the full range of idiomatic techniques that Stark will later define as constitutive of bel canto: dynamic gradation, legato, portamento, trills, ornamentation with good judgement, text clarity, and facial and gestural expression; Stark reads this as establishing that these techniques were in place before Caccini's codification.
**Type:** Direct Observation
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Bel Canto; Old Italian School

> [Stark, pp. 196–197]

**Claim:** Stark identifies Caccini as the most important single figure in establishing the old Italian school: his pupils, his preface to *Le nuove musiche*, and the Florentine style were recognised by contemporaries and by later authors from Praetorius (1619) through Stockhausen (1884) as the model for good singing.
**Type:** Interpretive Synthesis
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Bel Canto; Old Italian School

## The Castrato Phenomenon (pp. 197–204)

> [Stark, p. 198]

**Claim:** Stark explicitly rejects the claim that the castrati were "the most important element" in the development of bel canto; the principles of bel canto were established before the castrati rose to prominence, and the best technical descriptions were written when they were already in decline. He argues bel canto would have developed without them, while acknowledging that the castrati brought its ideals to an unprecedented level.
**Type:** Interpretive Synthesis
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Bel Canto; Castrati

> [Stark, p. 198, citing Milner 1973 and Celani]

**Claim:** The use of castrati in sacred choirs dates to at least 1553 (papal bull) and 1588–99 (Papal Choir records); at the court of Mantua, castrati played a decidedly secondary role to normal singers as late as 1607 (Monteverdi's *Orfeo*). Their dominance came only after the opening of the first public opera house in Venice in 1637.
**Type:** Direct Observation
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Castrati

> [Stark, pp. 200–204, synthesising Raguenet, Hawkins, Burney, Quantz, Mount-Edgcumbe]

**Claim:** Contemporary accounts of castrato singing consistently emphasise the same cluster of qualities: strong, penetrating, round, and sweet tone; astonishing flexibility combined with good judgement in ornamentation; clear articulation; extended range with united registers; exceptional trill execution; superb *messa di voce*; extraordinary breath economy; and the ability to move an audience by tone alone.
**Type:** Transmitted Tradition
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Castrati; Messa di voce; Bel Canto

> [Stark, pp. 203–204, citing Moses 1960 and Brodnitz 1975]

**Claim:** Stark presents Moses's psychoanalytic argument that castrato popularity arose from "wish fulfillment of hermaphroditic dreams" rooted in the mythological atmosphere of Baroque opera; he treats this as a serious if little-known interpretive contribution, while noting Brodnitz's parallel claim that "voices mirror the spirit of a period." Stark does not endorse the theory but presents it as the most developed answer to the question of why the phenomenon occurred.
**Type:** Speculative Extension
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Castrati

## National Differences in Singing Styles (pp. 204–216)

> [Stark, p. 204, citing Bontempi 1695 and Pleasants 1966]

**Claim:** Stark documents that Italian singing was treated as the universal standard against which French, German, and English singing were measured from the late 17th century onward; Pleasants identifies several "golden ages," all dominated by Italians or those trained in the Italian tradition.
**Type:** Historical Synthesis
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Bel Canto; Old Italian School

> [Stark, pp. 207–208, citing Bacilly 1668]

**Claim:** Bacilly, unusual among early authors, defended the falsetto voice in French singing against common prejudice, arguing that it could render ornamental devices impossible in the normal tenor voice; he preferred small, flexible voices with full tone quality over large ones, and acknowledged that the Italian language permitted greater musical freedom than French.
**Type:** Transmitted Tradition
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Register; Vocal Idiom

> [Stark, pp. 209–210, citing Quantz 1966, 328–329]

**Claim:** Quantz criticises French singing on technical grounds — specifically the failure to unite chest and falsetto registers, resulting in a "disagreeable shrieking" in the upper range — and contrasts this with Italian singing, which he describes as "profound and artful," moving the listener across passions. Stark uses Quantz and Burney as primary witnesses to the Italian-French technical distinction.
**Type:** Transmitted Tradition
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Register; Vocal Idiom

> [Stark, pp. 209, citing Zaslaw 1974 and Cyr 1977]

**Claim:** The French *haute-contre* was a high tenor who sang above the *passaggio* in full chest voice (not falsetto), reaching as high as B4–C5; Stark speculates — carefully flagging the conjecture — that the "forced and shouted" high notes of Jélyotte's successors may have been produced by belting, making this an early historical parallel to the modern belting phenomenon.
**Type:** Speculative Extension
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Belting; Register; Haute-contre

> [Stark, pp. 200–201, citing Bacon 1824]

**Claim:** Bacon identifies portamento as the most striking technical distinction between Italian and English singing: in constant use among Italians as an expressive device, it "appears not to accord with the genius of English expression." He also characterises Italian singing as theatrical, dramatic, and passionate; English singing as reserved, pure, and orchestral in sensibility.
**Type:** Transmitted Tradition
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Portamento; Vocal Idiom

> [Stark, p. 213]

**Claim:** Stark concludes the national styles section by asserting that bel canto was "truly an Italian affair"; other national traditions could emulate, imitate, or denigrate it, but the old Italian school was Italy's contribution to the world.
**Type:** Interpretive Synthesis
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Bel Canto; Old Italian School

## The Perennial "Decline of the Art of Singing" (pp. 213–225)

> [Stark, pp. 217–218, citing Shaw 1960, 329]

**Claim:** Stark frames every generation's complaint about declining singing as "the Good Old Times delusion" (Shaw's phrase) compounded by two real factors: the time lag between stylistic changes made by composers and the ability of singers and teachers to adapt, and the personal biases of commentators. He documents the complaint from Tosi (1723) through Garcia (1894) and Marchesi (1898).
**Type:** Interpretive Synthesis
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Bel Canto

> [Stark, pp. 232–236]

**Claim:** Stark identifies a structural "Catch-22" in the 18th-century complaints: light, flexible coloratura voices were acoustically incompatible with growing orchestras, but singers who sacrificed flexibility for a fuller tone were criticised for abandoning the old ways; the guidance in vocal treatises lagged behind compositional style by decades.
**Type:** Interpretive Synthesis
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Bel Canto; Vocal Idiom

> [Stark, pp. 261–262, citing G.B. Lamperti 1905, 31–35]

**Claim:** G.B. Lamperti directly contradicts his father and other "doomsayers" by arguing that the deterioration of singing is caused by insufficient training, not by Verdi or Wagner; a perfectly trained voice can sustain any style. He advocates four to five years of traditional study before public performance, and argues that Wagner himself subscribed to bel canto ideals.
**Type:** Prescriptive Technique
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Bel Canto; Appoggio

> [Stark, p. 266, citing Wagner 1834 essay "Pasticcio, by Canto Spianato"]

**Claim:** Stark demonstrates Wagner's own commitment to bel canto ideals through his 1834 essay praising "the old Italian mode of song" and criticising German composers for treating the voice as "a mere portion of the instrumental mass"; further evidenced by Wagner sending his niece Johanna to study with Garcia, and his 1876 invitation to Garcia to train the first Bayreuth Festival singers (declined by Garcia due to his London commitments).
**Type:** Direct Observation
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Bel Canto

## Coda: The "Secrets" of Bel Canto (pp. 225–226)

> [Stark, pp. 225–226, citing Klein 1923, Garcia 1894, and B. Marchesi 1923 and 1932]

**Claim:** Stark argues there are no lost secrets of bel canto: Garcia divulged all in 1841 and 1894. The "secrets" are the control and coordination of breath, the voice source (glottal closure settings), and the vocal tract — the same three elements that structure the entire book. He gives the final word to Blanche Marchesi: "if there ever was one who worked in the service of bel canto, adding to it the discovery of the actual secret vocal mechanism, it was he, Garcia the second."
**Type:** Interpretive Synthesis
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Bel Canto
