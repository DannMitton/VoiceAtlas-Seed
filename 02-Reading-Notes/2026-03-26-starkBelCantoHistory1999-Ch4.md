# Reading Notes: Stark Ch. 4 — Appoggio: The Breath Be Dammed!

<!-- p. 92 -->
**Context:** Chapter 4 opens with the historical dichotomy between breath inhalation and phonation. Stark notes the ambiguity of early quotes attributed to Pacchierotti and Holder.

> "Chi sa ben respirare e sillibare saprà ben cantare" (He who knows how to breathe and pronounce well, knows how to sing well). Source unclear, often ascribed to Gasparo Pacchierotti (1740–1821). Contrast with Dr. Holder (via Burney): "It is one thing to breathe, and another to vocalise that breath."

**Observation:** Stark leans toward Holder's view: the critical factor is how breath is turned into tone, not just the inhalation method. He notes that while posture and breathing methods matter, the ultimate control is phonation.

<!-- p. 93 -->
**Key Concept Definition:** Stark defines *appoggiare* ("to lean") as having two specific applications in singing:
1. **Muscular Antagonism:** The balance between inspiratory and expiratory muscles (often described as "bearing down" with the diaphragm).
2. **Laryngeal Role:** The larynx "holding back" or "damming" the breath via glottal resistance and intentional lowering against upward pressure.

> [Stark, p. 93]

**Claim:** *Appoggio* is a complex equilibrium between respiratory and laryngeal muscles, functioning as a metaphor of "leaning" on the breath.
**Type:** Transmitted Tradition
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Appoggio

<!-- p. 94 -->
**Historical Survey (Early Treatises):**
- **Maffei (1562):** Advocated pressing breath with the voice; noted falsetto uses more breath.
- **Zacconi (1592):** Linked breath control to phrasing; advised taking only as many ornaments as possible on one breath.
- **Bovicelli (1594):** First to advocate *silent inhalation*; mocked singers who breathe too often ("like a horse afraid of its shadow").
- **Caccini (1602):** Focused on breath economy and the *crescendo-decrescendo* (swelling/diminishing). Warned against falsetto waste.
- **Mazzocchi (1638):** Described increasing "liveliness but not tone" (ambiguous).
- **Bacilly (1668):** Noted breath control is acquired through training, not just lung capacity.

**Theme:** Early texts focused on economy, silence, and phrasing rather than complex mechanics.

<!-- p. 95 -->
**Eighteenth-Century Tutors:**
- **Tosi (1743):** Declared *messa di voce* in decline. Defined it as soft-to-forte-to-soft on open vowels.
- **Mancini (1774):** Emphasised conserving breath. Linked *messa di voce* mastery to breath management ("taking back the breath"). Praised Farinelli for invisible breath control.
- **Martini (1792):** "Holding back" air to swell/diminish tones. Precursor to *appoggio*.
- **Crescentini (1810):** Defined *messa di voce* as effortless attack/strengthening/diminishing on single notes. Contrasted with Garcia's "equal force" tones.

<!-- p. 96 -->
**The Garcia School:**
- **Garaudé (1830):** Absorbed Italian practices. *Messa di voce* = "filé" (spun tone), even timbre, no register change, no *voce di testa*.
- **Garcia (1841/1847):**
  - **Inhalation:** Quiet breath, raised chest + lowered diaphragm. Rejected exclusive abdominal breathing.
  - **Phonation:** Continuous diaphragm pressure ("steady, moderate, prolonged").
  - **Messa di Voce:** Required register/timbre change (falsetto → chest → falsetto). Involved larynx lowering for dark timbre, then raising for brilliance.
  - **Conflict:** Garcia's method (register change) vs. Garaudé/Lamperti (even timbre).

<!-- p. 97 -->
**Garcia's Student:**
- **Stockhausen (1884):** Echoed Garcia on inhalation. Surprised Garcia placed *messa di voce* late in the treatise; Stockhausen viewed it as a *beginning* exercise.

<!-- p. 98 -->
**Hermann Klein (1856–1934):**
- Student of Garcia.
- Silent breathing = low larynx.
- Advocated "compressed breath" and *messa di voce* as central to old Italian school.
- Quoted Garcia: "Repress breathing power... proportion with resisting force of throat."

<!-- p. 99 -->
**The Mandl-Lamperti School:**
- **Dr. Louis Mandl (1876):** Coined *lutte vocale* ("vocal struggle"). Defined as balance between inspiratory (retaining air) and expiratory (driving air) muscles.
- **Francesco Lamperti:**
  - Adopted Mandl's *lutte vocale*.
  - **Breathing:** Diaphragmatic only. "Belly out." Avoid chest-rib effort (elastic recoil causes glottal closure/larynx rise).
  - **Airflow:** Low rate (candle test: flame must not flicker).
  - **Pressure:** "Voice must be less vigorous than the breath."
  - **Appoggio Definition:** "Singing appoggiata" = perfect command of air column; holding back breath; "slight back-stroke of the glottis" (as if continuing to inhale).
  - **Metaphor:** "Drinking the tone." Sounds reflected in back of head.
  - **Scope:** *Appoggio* covers onset, glottal closure, larynx position, airflow, legato, *messa di voce*, and intonation. It is the "great secret."

<!-- p. 100 -->
**Giovanni Battista Lamperti:**
- Combined diaphragmatic breathing with intercostal action for "compressed breath."
- **Imagery:** "When top and bottom of lungs are equally full of compressed air, voice focuses in head."
- **Mechanism:** Voice controls breath, not reverse. Breath held back by vibration opposing air exit + body restraining energy. Diaphragm acts as "stop-cock."
- **Pressure:** Steady, elevated subglottal pressure required for *all* notes (soft and loud).
- **Proprioception:** Relied on "inside skin" (mucous membrane nerves) to feel cavities.
- **Messa di Voce:** "Most difficult problem." Produced solely by breath control. No register change. "Singing loudly is releasing; singing softly is restraining pent-up energy."

<!-- p. 101 -->
**William Shakespeare (Student of F. Lamperti):**
- Combined diaphragmatic + rib breathing.
- **Appoggio:** Powerful pressure regulated by balancing rib muscles against diaphragm/abdominals.
- **Efficiency:** "Less breath produces more sound." Rejected loose glottal closure.
- **Messa di Voce:** Followed Garcia (undefined register change).

<!-- p. 102 -->
**Modern Synthesis:**
- **Franklyn Kelsey (1954):** *Appoggio* = "leaning upon breath" at top of windpipe. "Respiratory squeeze."
- **Franziska Martienssen-Lohmann (1956):** *Appoggio* = *Ganzheit* (total system). Synonymous with good singing.
- **Richard Miller:** *Appoggio* = total system (support + resonance).

<!-- p. 103 -->
**Stauprinzip (German "Stemming Principle"):**
- **Context:** Late 19th/early 20th century. Response to Wagnerian demands (power over orchestra).
- **Proponents:** Armin, Schilling, Luchsinger, Arnold.
- **Definition:** Extreme version of *appoggio*. High subglottal pressure, strong glottal resistance ("painful groan" or *Stönlaut*).
- **Opponents:** Paul Bruns (*Minimalluft*).
  - Argued *Stütze* = "pumped-full lungs."
  - Advocated *Freilauf* ("free flow") with residual air, mobile diaphragm, light production.
  - Blamed Wagner for the "war" between voice and orchestra.
- **Caruso Debate:** Both sides claimed Caruso. Fucito (Caruso's coach) noted Caruso used perfect control of *every particle* of outgoing air, not residual air.

<!-- p. 104 -->
**Modern Science (Breath Mechanics):**
- **Respiratory System:** Rib cage + diaphragm + abdomen.
- **Inhalation:** Diaphragm flattens (bulges abdomen); ribs raise.
- **Exhalation:** Abdominal/internal intercostal contraction reduces space.
- **Controversy:** Passive recoil vs. Active muscular force.
  - *Edinburgh Group (Leanderson/Sundberg):* Passive recoil initiates, then active muscle takes over. Constant pressure requires changing muscle force as lung volume drops.
  - *Hixon/Weismer:* Challenged passive recoil dominance; emphasised active abdominal effort.
  - *Proctor:* Diaphragm is largely passive during phonation.
  - *Vennard/Lamperti view:* Diaphragm is active antagonist (brake).
- **Consensus (Pedagogical):** Active antagonism ("brakes and gas pedal" simultaneously) from pre-phonation to end of phrase.

<!-- p. 105 -->
**Subglottal Pressure & Airflow:**
- **Pressure:** Measured in cmH₂O.
  - Threshold phonation: ~2 cmH₂O.
  - Speech: 7–12 cmH₂O.
  - Singing: 5–20 cmH₂O (typical); up to 40–70 cmH₂O (loud); tenors >100 cmH₂O on high notes.
- **Airflow:** Measured in ml/s.
  - Falsetto = high airflow.
  - Strong closure = low airflow.
  - Vibrato = ~10% more breath loss.
- **Efficiency:** Aesthetic demands often trump efficiency.

<!-- p. 106 -->
**Messa di Voce (Science View):**
- **Two Methods:**
  1. **Garcia:** Register/timbre change (falsetto → chest → falsetto).
  2. **Garaudé/Lamperti:** Even timbre, no register change.
- **Titze:** Confirms both exist. Method 1 = progressive adduction. Method 2 = steady adduction.

<!-- p. 107 -->
**Neuromuscular Control:**
- **Breath Impulse:** Triggered by CO₂ accumulation, not O₂ need.
- **CO₂ Tolerance:** Singers train to resist urge to breathe.
- **Proprioception:** Abdominal/intercostal muscles rich in spindles (sensors). Diaphragm has few.
- **Reflexes:** Mucosal pressure receptors in larynx help fine-tune pressure.

<!-- p. 108 -->
**Stark's Conclusion:**
- **Appoggio as Ganzheit:** A total system of physical adjustments.
- **Subjective Feeling:** Elastic "cushion" of pressurised breath below glottis. Adds buoyancy, malleability, and projection.
- **Conditions:**
  1. **Inhalation:** Low breath (relaxed abdominals → low diaphragm → low larynx). Prevents "clutching."
  2. **Pre-phonation:** Glottis firmly closed. Larynx low.
  3. **Phonation:** Expiratory muscles elevate pressure *without* raising larynx.
  4. **Onset:** Firm adduction, large closed quotient. Avoid plosive.
  5. **Sustain:** Constant interplay of pressure, resistance, and vocal tract.
- **Result:** Seamless legato, controlled vibrato, no register breaks.
