# Reading Notes: Stark Ch. 2 — Chiaroscuro: The Tractable Tract

<!-- p. 33 -->
**Chiaroscuro as Ideal Voice Quality:**
- The term *chiaroscuro* ("bright-dark" tone) was the tonal ideal for classically trained singers in the 18th and 19th centuries.
- First used in a pedagogical context by Mancini (1774): scales practised to achieve "that true expression which forms the cantilena colored with chiaroscuro."
- G.B. Lamperti: "The 'dark-light' tone should be always present" (W.E. Brown 1957, 38–9).
- Richard Miller: *chiaroscuro* designates "that basic timbre of the singing voice in which the laryngeal source and the resonating system appear to interact" to produce the quality the singer calls "resonant" (Transcripts 1983, 2:135).

> [Stark, p. 33–34]

**Claim:** Chiaroscuro — the simultaneous presence of bright and dark tonal qualities — was the dominant tonal ideal of classical singing from at least the 18th century, and remains a defining feature of operatically trained voices.
**Type:** Transmitted Tradition
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Chiaroscuro

<!-- p. 34–36 -->
**The Early Treatises:**
- Zacconi (1592): preferred the "biting" (*mordente*) chest voice over dull or shrill voices.
- Caccini (1602): advocated *voce piena e naturale* (full and natural voice), rejecting falsetto (*le voci finte*).
- Praetorius (1619) and Herbst (1642): transmitted Caccini's preference for bright chest voice into German pedagogy.
- Garaudé (c.1830): preferred chest voice with "round and sonorous" quality.

**Theme:** From the outset of virtuoso solo singing, chest voice was preferred over falsetto for its bright, biting quality; combined with roundness, this anticipates *chiaroscuro*.

<!-- p. 36–40 -->
**Garcia's Theory — Voice Source vs. Vocal Tract:**
- Garcia was the first to systematically distinguish between the glottal source and the vocal tract as separate contributors to tone quality.
- Ringing tone (*voix éclatante*) = firm glottal closure; veiled tone (*voix sourde*) = loose closure.
- *Timbre clair* (clear timbre) = high larynx; *timbre sombre* (dark timbre) = lowered larynx and widened pharynx.
- Garcia's "purest tone": *éclat* (brightness from glottal closure) combined with *rondeur* (roundness from low larynx and expanded pharynx).
- Garcia did not use the word *chiaroscuro*, but his vocal ideal is consistent with it.

> [Stark, pp. 36–40]

**Claim:** Garcia was the first to distinguish the glottal source (producing brightness via firm closure) from the vocal tract (producing darkness via laryngeal lowering and pharyngeal expansion) as separate but coordinated contributors to ideal tone quality.
**Type:** Historical Synthesis
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Chiaroscuro

<!-- p. 41–42 -->
**Voix sombrée ou couverte:**
- Gilbert-Louis Duprez (1806–96): first tenor celebrated for singing high C5 (*ut de poitrine*) in chest voice using *voix sombrée ou couverte*.
- Premiered this in Rossini's *Guillaume Tell*, Paris, 1837.
- Garcia's treatment of *voix sombrée* was far broader than Diday and Pétrequin's: he described the lowered larynx across all registers and dynamics, not only on high notes.

<!-- p. 42–45 -->
**Voce aperta and Voce chiusa (Lamperti School):**
- Francesco Lamperti: advocated *timbro aperto* as the training timbre; warned against *voce bianca* (white tone, caused by insufficient glottal resistance).
- G.B. Lamperti: "In their inception all tones are dark to be opened or closed at will. This muted beginning evolves into the 'dark-light tone' which is the ideal quality of the human voice" (W.E. Brown 1957, 37).
- Richard Miller: *voce chiusa* (closed voice) = stabilised low larynx + widened pharynx + vowel modification (*aggiustamento*) = "covered" sound in upper range (R. Miller 1986a, 151).

> [Stark, pp. 42–45]

**Claim:** The Lamperti school's *voce chiusa* / *voce aperta* terminology describes the same tonal balance as *chiaroscuro*: a dark foundation (low larynx, expanded pharynx) opened sufficiently to produce brightness, avoiding *voce bianca*.
**Type:** Interpretive Synthesis
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Chiaroscuro

<!-- p. 45–51 -->
**Helmholtz and Modern Formant Theory:**
- Helmholtz (1862): established that vocal tone consists of a fundamental and upper partials; noted a high-frequency energy peak in the human voice unique among musical instruments.
- Fant (1960): *source-filter theory* — glottis produces harmonic series; vocal tract filters it via formants (F1, F2, etc.).
- Singer's formant: resonance cluster at ~2500–3500 Hz; enables the singer to be heard over an orchestra (orchestra's peak energy is ~450 Hz).
- Dependent on abrupt and complete glottal closure in each cycle.
- Low larynx darkens tone by lowering all formant frequencies, especially F1.
- Tenor high notes: some tenors use F2 tuning rather than singer's formant (Schutte and Miller 1996).
- Soprano high notes: F1 tuned to H1 from F5 upward; sometimes called the "bloom" of the voice.

> [Stark, pp. 49–51]

**Claim:** The singer's formant (2500–3500 Hz) is the acoustical mechanism underlying the "brightness" component of *chiaroscuro*; it depends on firm glottal closure and is largely independent of vowel or dynamic level.
**Type:** Empirical Finding
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Chiaroscuro; Singer's Formant

<!-- p. 51–55 -->
**Resonance Imagery:**
- Traditional pedagogy relied heavily on imagery: "placing" the voice, "singing dans le masque," directing tone to sinuses, palate, teeth, etc.
- Garcia explicitly rejected resonance imagery.
- Both Lampertis used imagery pragmatically but acknowledged its illusory nature.
- Francesco: "Strictly speaking there are no such things as nasal voice, head voice, chest voice ... all voice is generated in the throat" (F. Lamperti 1884, 17).
- Titze: some resonance sensations may correspond to genuine pressure maxima in the vocal tract (Titze 1994, 167) — partial scientific rehabilitation.
- Sundberg: nasal cavity and head/chest resonances "not relevant to the major acoustic properties of the vowels produced in professional operatic singing" (Sundberg 1977a, 91).

> [Stark, pp. 51–55]

**Claim:** Resonance imagery is a pedagogically widespread but physiologically inaccurate framework; it may nonetheless function as effective indirect instruction by eliciting correct acoustic results through misdirection.
**Type:** Useful Metaphor
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Resonance Imagery

<!-- p. 55–56 -->
**Conclusions (Stark's Summary):**
- Firm glottal closure = bright element (rich high-frequency source).
- Low larynx + expanded pharynx = dark element (lowered formants, especially F1).
- Both can be produced simultaneously: *chiaroscuro*.
- Garcia was the first to theorise the source-filter distinction in pedagogy.
- Helmholtz established the acoustical foundations (source-filter theory).
- *Chiaroscuro* and formant tuning are "a trademark of the old Italian school of singing."
