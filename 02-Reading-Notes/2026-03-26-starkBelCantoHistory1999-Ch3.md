# Reading Notes: Stark Ch. 3 — Registers: Some Tough Breaks

<!-- p. 57–58 -->
**The Problem of Registers:**
- The voice has natural physiological and acoustical discontinuities as it ascends from lowest to highest notes: these are registers.
- Old Italian school: two registers only — chest voice and head voice.
- 18th century: orthodoxy disturbed by three-register theories.
- Garcia (1841): proposed a rapprochement between two- and three-register theories; remained the reference point ever since.
- Register transitions are called "breaks." The old Italian school advocated equalising registers but offered little practical advice on how.

<!-- p. 58–63 -->
**Two-Register Theory of the Old Italian School:**
- Zacconi (1592): *voce di petto* (chest) preferred over *voce di testa* (head/falsetto); chest voice = more power, better intonation, more delight.
- Caccini (1602): *voce piena e naturale* (chest) vs. *voce finta* (falsetto); falsetto rejected for lack of nobility, breathiness, inability to make strong dynamic contrasts.
- Tosi (1723): two registers must be united; head voice best for florid music and trills but weaker than chest; urged equalising them so "one cannot distinguish the one from the other."
- Mancini (1774): "of all the difficulties that one encounters in the art of singing, the greatest by far is the union of the two registers." Offered advice to hold back the chest voice and strengthen the head voice, but did not specify how.

> [Stark, pp. 58–63]

**Claim:** The two-register theory (chest and head/falsetto) was the orthodox framework of the old Italian school from Zacconi onward; the unification of these registers was considered the supreme pedagogical challenge, but early treatises offered little specific technical guidance on how to achieve it.
**Type:** Transmitted Tradition
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Registers; Passaggio

<!-- p. 64–67 -->
**Countercurrents to the Two-Register Theory:**
- Galliard (Tosi's English translator, 1743): proposed three registers — chest, head voice, and falsetto as distinct.
- Agricola (Tosi's German translator, 1757): elaborate and confused multi-register theory; distinguished high falsetto from head voice by high larynx position; his low "falsetto" was likely pulse register.
- Nathan (1836): four registers — chest, falsetto (throat voice), head voice, and "feigned voice" (*voce finta*) as a transitional bridge.
- Late 18th century: several authors proposed three registers for female voices specifically.

<!-- p. 67–76 -->
**Garcia's Theory of the Main Registers:**
- Garcia's definition (1840/1841): "a series of consecutive and homogeneous tones going from low to high, produced by the development of the same mechanical principle."
- Two main registers in his *Mémoire*: chest (*registre de poitrine*) and falsetto-head (*registre de fausset-tête*), the latter comprising falsetto (lower portion) and head (upper portion).
- Two mechanical principles: (1) mode of vibration — full fold body in chest, inner margins only in falsetto; (2) glottal closure — firm in chest and head, loose in falsetto.
- Later (in *Hints on Singing*, 1894) Garcia adopted three registers: chest, medium, and head — "These names are incorrect, but accepted."
- Key to equalising registers: firm glottal closure (*coup de la glotte*, "pinching the glottis") to overcome the weakness of the falsetto/medium.
- Garcia also described *mezza voce*: extension of chest register upward using loose phonation and relaxed pharynx; weak in high-frequency components, perceived as a quiet "half-voice."
- Covered high notes (*ut de poitrine*): high subglottal pressure, strong contraction of glottal and vocal tract muscles, strong high-frequency components; Garcia did not recommend this approach, warning of "exhaustion and paralysis."

> [Stark, pp. 67–76]

**Claim:** Garcia's register theory rested on two distinct mechanical principles — mode of vocal fold vibration and degree of glottal closure — and his prescribed remedy for register breaks was firm glottal adduction, not resonance adjustment.
**Type:** Historical Synthesis
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Registers; Passaggio; Coup de la glotte

<!-- p. 73–75 -->
**Voix mixte and Mezza voce:**
- Before Duprez, tenors sang high notes in *voix mixte* (mixed voice) or *voce di mezzo petto* (half-chest voice) — a light emission between chest and head.
- Garaudé (1830): *voix mixte* allows tenor to extend chest voice upward "without changing the register."
- Garcia rejected the term *voix mixte* as physiologically impossible.
- Garcia described *mezza voce* as an extension of chest register with loose phonation and relaxed pharynx — not a blend of registers but a modification of one.
- Rossini disliked Duprez's covered high C, reportedly calling it "the squawk of a capon whose throat is being cut."

<!-- p. 76–81 -->
**Aftermath of Garcia's Register Theory:**
- Mathilde Marchesi: emphatically maintained three registers in female voices; advised "closing" the upper notes of each register when ascending as a means of equalising — interpreted by Large and Murray (1978) as medial compression.
- Stockhausen: followed Garcia's two modes of vibration; three registers; advocated fixed larynx on transitional notes; *sombre* quality ascending, *clair* quality descending.
- Emma Seiler: five-register theory — two subdivisions of chest and two of falsetto, plus head; largely a recasting of Garcia's two mechanical principles.
- Lampertis: treated registers, timbres, and breathing as a whole rather than separate techniques; Francesco preferred "mixed" to "falsetto" for the middle register; Giovanni added a fourth register for male voices — *voix mixte* — and attributed the scarcity of eminent tenors partly to its neglect.
- G.B. Lamperti: "The vocal registers are determined by the different points of resonance of the tones" — a resonance-imagery framing, not a mechanical one.

<!-- p. 81–88 -->
**Register Theory and Modern Voice Science:**
- Most widely accepted view: two primary registers (laryngeal), plus secondary acoustic registers and auxiliary registers at extremes.
- Van den Berg (1960): two primary registers caused by "active" vs. "passive" longitudinal tension; maximum chest voice approximately Eb4; register break at that point; overlap of about one octave (Eb3–Eb4) where either register is possible.
- Rubin and Hirt (1960): high-speed film confirmed — chest voice = full fold body vibrating; falsetto = margins only, barely touching; consistent with Garcia's laryngoscopic observations.
- Lower register: richer in high-frequency components, more efficient (lower airflow, higher subglottal pressure, greater intensity).
- *Passaggio*: generally accepted at about Eb4 in both sexes; Richard Miller describes *primo passaggio*, *secondo passaggio*, and *zona di passaggio* in male voices, though most sources discuss only one passaggio in males.

> [Stark, pp. 82–83]

**Claim:** Modern voice science confirms Garcia's two-register framework: chest voice involves full vocal fold vibration with active longitudinal tension; falsetto involves margin-only vibration with passive tension. The break typically occurs near Eb4 in both sexes.
**Type:** Empirical Finding
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Registers; Passaggio

<!-- p. 84–86 -->
**Covered Singing:**
- Covering = lowering larynx and expanding pharynx above the *passaggio*; delays register shift; allows chest register to function beyond normal limits.
- Sonninen (1956): lowered larynx via sternothyroid muscle frees cricothyroid to contract through full range, reducing effective vibrating mass and delaying register shift.
- Tenors: covering begins around F#4; extends chest register to C5 (*do di petto*). Baritones: approximately Eb4.
- At the *passaggio*, dominant resonance shifts from F1-H2 tuning to either singer's formant cluster or F2-H2/H3 alignment.
- Also known as: *voce piena in testa* (Italian), *Vollton der Kopfstimme* (German), "legitimate head voice" (Richard Miller).

> [Stark, pp. 84–85]

**Claim:** Covered singing is a vocal tract manoeuvre — lowered larynx, expanded pharynx — that delays the register break and extends the chest register upward; it is accompanied by a shift in dominant resonance strategy at the *passaggio*.
**Type:** Empirical Finding
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Covered Singing; Passaggio; Registers

<!-- p. 85–86 -->
**Belting:**
- Chest voice carried above its normal switching point (approximately E4); larynx rises (contrast with covering, where larynx descends).
- Large closed quotient, increased subglottal pressure, high effort, muscle rigidity.
- Often produces tone without vibrato, or "vibrato crescendo."
- Schutte and Miller definition: "loud singing characterized by consistent use of chest register (>50% closed phase) in a range where larynx elevation is necessary to match F1 with H2 on open vowels."
- Covering vs. belting: same register extension, opposite laryngeal strategy.
- More research needed before wholesale condemnation; some belters have long careers without injury.

> [Stark, pp. 85–86]

**Claim:** Belting and covering are both extensions of the chest register above the passaggio, but are acoustically and physiologically opposite strategies: covering lowers the larynx; belting raises it.
**Type:** Empirical Finding
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Belting; Covered Singing; Registers

<!-- p. 86–89 -->
**Mixed and Acoustic Registers:**
- Van den Berg: "mid-voice" is a mixture of chest and falsetto, occurring between the zones of active and passive longitudinal tension.
- D.G. Miller: simultaneous activation of chest and falsetto modes is "experimentally feasible" but "not applicable in singing"; register blending is achieved by other means.
- Large: register blending achieved by firm arytenoid closure preventing a glottal "chink" in the middle register — consistent with Garcia's "glottal pinch."
- Acoustic registers: "lifts" caused by shifts in dominant resonance (formant tuning shifts), not laryngeal mechanism changes; distinct from laryngeal registers.
- Key lift in female voice: approximately D5, shift from F2-H2 to F1-H1 tuning; perceived as lightening of tone or entry into "head voice."

<!-- p. 89–90 -->
**Auxiliary Registers:**
- *Strohbass* / vocal fry / pulse register: low auxiliary register; crackling quality; large fold amplitudes, small closed quotient, low airflow and subglottal pressure.
- Flageolet / whistle / flute register: high soprano auxiliary; begins between B4–Eb5; characterised by resonance shift, not change in sound source; minimal vocal fold oscillation; vowel identification lost.

<!-- p. 90 -->
**Stark's Conclusions:**
- Garcia was correct: registers arise from different mechanical principles.
- Modern voice science identifies several such principles: mode of fold vibration (chest, falsetto, *Strohbass*, flageolet); glottal closure settings; vocal tract adjustments ("lifts" and covering).
- Garcia's definition remains a "good starting point." Modern science has refined it without superseding it.
