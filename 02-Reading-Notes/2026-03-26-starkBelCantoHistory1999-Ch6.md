## Problems of Idiom and Expression (pp. 153–154)

> "Expressivity in singing can be strongly linked not only to the structure of the music or the meanings of the texts, but also to the manner in which the voice itself is used." (p. 154)

**Claim:** Stark argues that vocal technique is an autonomous expressive resource, distinct from text and musical structure, and that the pedagogy literature is the only literature that addresses this dimension directly.
**Type:** Interpretive Synthesis
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Vocal Idiom

> "By including the vocal element in musical analysis we can arrive at a 'word-note-tone relationship,' which offers a better (although still incomplete) measure of musical expressivity." (p. 154)

**Claim:** Stark proposes the "word-note-tone relationship" as an analytical framework that adds the cultivated singing voice to the conventional word-tone model; he attributes neglect of this dimension to musicology's score-based analytical habits.
**Type:** Interpretive Synthesis
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Vocal Idiom

## The Art of Diminutions (pp. 154–156)

> "The florid vocal patterns used for ornamenting polyphonic song were known as 'diminutions' (from diminuire), in which long notes were broken up into passages of smaller notes." (p. 155)

**Claim:** Stark defines diminutions as the subdivision of long notes into shorter ornamental passages; he identifies the main patterns as tirata (ascending scale), cascata (descending), and circolo mezzo (arc returning to the same pitch).
**Type:** Direct Observation
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Diminution

> "In order to perform these ornaments well, the singer required a good disposizione della gorga (disposition of the throat) or disposizione della voce (disposition of the voice) which indicated a particularly supple throat for singing gorgie. This was often regarded more as a gift of nature than a trainable skill." (p. 155)

**Claim:** Early sources treated the throat agility required for diminutions (disposizione della gorga) as a natural gift rather than an acquired skill; Stark notes this view persisted into later debates about the trill.
**Type:** Transmitted Tradition
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Diminution

> [Earliest vocal manuals of diminution listed] Giovanni Camillo Maffei (1562), Girolamo dalla Casa (1584), Giovanni Battista Bassani (1585), Giovanni Luca Conforto (1593), Giovanni Battista Bovicelli (1594); preceded by Ganassi for recorder (1534) and Ortiz for violin (1553). (pp. 155–156)

**Claim:** Stark documents that the earliest printed manuals for vocal diminution postdate instrumental manuals by several decades, with Maffei (1562) as the earliest surviving vocal source.
**Type:** Direct Observation
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Diminution

## Caccini's Affective Vocal Devices (pp. 156–163)

> Caccini rejected the "old style of passaggi formerly in common use (one more suited to wind and stringed instruments than to the voice)," finding them "nothing more inimical to affective expression." (p. 157, citing Caccini 1970, 47)

**Claim:** Caccini's Le nuove musiche explicitly rejects indiscriminate passaggi on the grounds that they originate in instrumental idiom and undermine text expression; this is one of the earliest recorded arguments that singing technique must be idiomatic to the voice.
**Type:** Transmitted Tradition
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Passaggi; Vocal Idiom

> "Caccini said the chief requirements for expressive singing were a good voice and good breath control, which together enabled the singer to produce all the devices of affective singing. A 'good voice' excluded the falsetto (voce finta), which he said was wasteful of breath and 'often [became] harsh and unbearable to the ear when singing a crescendo.'" (p. 158, citing Caccini 1970, 56, 49)

**Claim:** Caccini identifies voce piena e naturale (full, natural voice) as the prerequisite for affective singing, explicitly excluding the voce finta on grounds of breath inefficiency and timbral harshness on crescendo.
**Type:** Prescriptive Technique
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Voce Piena e Naturale

> "Caccini illustrated a number of specific vocal devices necessary for affective singing. One of these was the intonazione della voce, in which the first note of the phrase was begun about a third below the written note." (p. 158)

**Claim:** Caccini describes the intonazione della voce as a low pitch onset device beginning approximately a third below the target pitch; Stark identifies parallel descriptions in Zacconi, Bovicelli, Praetorius, Bernhard, Agricola (Schleifer), and Montéclair (son glissé), suggesting widespread early practice.
**Type:** Transmitted Tradition
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Intonazione della voce; Low Pitch Onset

> "The esclamazione... also seems to involve matters of vocal relaxation vs. intensity (having little to do with dynamics) which cannot be represented graphically." (p. 159, citing Hitchcock)

**Claim:** Stark, following Hitchcock, interprets Caccini's esclamazione as involving both an intensity change and a shift in voice quality (not dynamics alone), and proposes that Garcia's description of the messa di voce (intensity change plus veiled-to-bright quality shift) may offer the closest functional analogue.
**Type:** Interpretive Synthesis
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Esclamazione; Messa di voce

> [Caccini's tension-and-release pattern:] "he lengthened certain notes beyond their written value, and often tied them to the next note, as a method of building vocal and musical tension. This lengthened tone was followed by an urgent vocal release — a tirata, a cascata, a gruppo, a trillo, or a ribattuta di gola." (p. 160)

**Claim:** Stark identifies a structural pattern in Caccini's affective style: sustained tension on a prolonged note, released into a vocal flourish; he reads this tension-and-release pattern as the central affective mechanism of Caccini's idiom.
**Type:** Interpretive Synthesis
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Vocal Idiom; Sprezzatura

## Meraviglia, Sprezzatura, and Grazia (pp. 160–162)

> "Caccini's affective singing style was further characterized by sprezzatura, a rhythmic flexibility that allowed for the natural accentuation of the words, and a departure from Renaissance rules of part-writing so as to create dissonances that enhanced the expression of the words." (pp. 160–161)

**Claim:** Stark defines sprezzatura in Caccini as a dual concept: rhythmic freedom following the natural accentuation of text, and deliberate harmonic licence (dissonance against the bass). He traces the term to Castiglione's Il libro del cortegiano (1528).
**Type:** Direct Observation
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Sprezzatura

> "The quality of grazia in singing was closely related to the rhythmic subtleties of sprezzatura... grazia... went beyond mere beauty by including technical effortlessness (facilità), sweetness (dolcezza), and softness (morbidezza). It was a natural gift that could only spring from 'an effortless, unforced manner.'" (p. 162)

**Claim:** Stark distinguishes grazia from mere beauty: it encompasses technical effortlessness, sweetness, and softness, aligned with sprezzatura's quality of apparent nonchalance; both are identified as defining characteristics of early Baroque expressive singing.
**Type:** Direct Observation
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Grazia; Sprezzatura

## Voice Quality, Appoggio, and Vibrato (pp. 163–164)

> "Garcia said that timbres and breathing 'form an inarticulate language, composed of tears, interjections, cries, sighs, etc., which one could properly name the language of the soul.'" (p. 163, citing Garcia 1975, 138–65)

**Claim:** Garcia frames vocal timbre and breath as a prelinguistic emotional language; Stark uses this to anchor his argument that chiaroscuro, register equalisation, appoggio, and vibrato are expressive resources and not merely acoustic facts.
**Type:** Transmitted Tradition
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Chiaroscuro; Appoggio; Vibrato

> "Francesco Lamperti maintained that 'expression is wholly wanting in a voice not appoggiata.' He considered appoggio absolutely essential in order to 'convey the emotions of the soul or express the various feelings of the human passions.'" (p. 164, citing F. Lamperti)

**Claim:** F. Lamperti asserts that expressive singing is impossible without appoggio; Stark uses this to support his argument that breath management and voice quality are inseparable from expressive function in the classical tradition.
**Type:** Prescriptive Technique
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Appoggio

**Claim:** Despite his general wariness of vibrato, Garcia acknowledged its expressive value when used contextually and sparingly; Stark highlights this to complicate the common reading of Garcia as simply anti-vibrato.
**Type:** Interpretive Synthesis

## Vocal Articulation: Low Pitch Onset (pp. 164–165)

> "Low pitch onset is still practiced by singers today... Francesco Lamperti called this error striciato... Today it is called 'scooping' (Michel 1983)." (p. 165)

**Claim:** Stark traces the modern term "scooping" to Caccini's intonazione della voce; F. Lamperti rejected the exaggerated form (striciato) while Stark and Sundberg identify functional grounds for controlled low onset: vowel intelligibility and laryngeal stabilisation.
**Type:** Transmitted Tradition
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Low Pitch Onset; Intonazione della voce

> "Modern researchers have shown that the vocal 'sweeping' action associated with low pitch onset helps to convey more linguistic information than is possible with a dead-on pitch... Johan Sundberg reported using low pitch onset... in synthesized singing, where he maintained it played an expressive role (Sundberg 1985a, 102–3)." (p. 165)

**Claim:** Sundberg's acoustic research provides an empirical basis for low pitch onset: the ascending sweep of fundamental frequency and harmonics may increase vowel formant contact and enhance intelligibility; Stark also suggests it helps singers establish low laryngeal position before approaching a high note.
**Type:** Empirical Finding
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Low Pitch Onset

## Legato (pp. 165–166)

> "Francesco Lamperti stated categorically, 'Without legato there is no singing (chi non lega non canta)'; and, 'the two things that render the human voice superior to every other instrument are the power of legato, and the variation in colour.'" (pp. 165–166, citing F. Lamperti 1884, 21)

**Claim:** F. Lamperti elevates legato to the defining capacity of the singing voice; he pairs it with timbral flexibility as the two supreme vocal advantages over instruments.
**Type:** Prescriptive Technique
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Legato

> "G.B. Lamperti said the pupil must 'observe a strict legato, a smooth and unbroken passage from one tone to the other. The breathing must not be interrupted between the tones, but flow evenly as if a single tone were sung.'" (p. 165, citing G.B. Lamperti 1905, 12; W.E. Brown 1957, 6)

**Claim:** G.B. Lamperti defines legato as uninterrupted breath flow between tones; Stark adds that true legato also depends on register equalisation, controlled subglottal breath pressure, stable laryngeal position, and the singer's formant.
**Type:** Prescriptive Technique
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Legato

> "Garcia called this the 'breadth or holding of the voice on the words' (largeur ou tenue de la voix sur les paroles), and maintained that the singer must be able to separate the functions of various organs in order to create legato with words." (p. 166, citing Garcia 1847, 2:7)

**Claim:** Garcia identifies organ independence — the ability to modify pitch, vowel, and intensity independently — as a prerequisite for true legato in texted singing.
**Type:** Prescriptive Technique
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Legato

## Portamento (pp. 166–167)

> "Mancini (1774)... wrote, 'By this portamento is meant nothing but a passing, tying the voice, from one note to the next with perfect proportion and union... a just and limpid gradation, which should be maintained and tied in the passage from one note to another.' He also noted that the portamento 'cannot be acquired by any scholar who has not already united the two registers.'" (pp. 166–167, citing Mancini 1967, 40–41)

**Claim:** Mancini defines portamento as a smooth, proportional glide between pitches requiring prior register unification; this directly links portamento to the Atlas nodes for register and passaggio.
**Type:** Prescriptive Technique
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Portamento; Register

> "G.B. Lamperti added one further piece of advice when he said that, as the voice curves upward or downward, 'the appoggio remains unmoved,' thereby linking appoggio to portamento." (p. 167, citing W.E. Brown 1957, 21)

**Claim:** G.B. Lamperti explicitly links appoggio to portamento, asserting that the breath support mechanism must remain stable during pitch slides; this is a direct pedagogical claim connecting two Atlas nodes.
**Type:** Prescriptive Technique
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Appoggio; Portamento

## Floridity: Canto Fiorito (pp. 167–169)

> "Garcia recognized that light and heavy voices sang florid passages in different ways... 'agility itself is of two different natures: di forza (of power) and di maniera (of dexterity). These two natures are rarely found together in the same voice.'" (pp. 168–169, citing Garcia 1984, 197)

**Claim:** Garcia distinguishes two physiologically distinct types of vocal agility: di forza (requiring higher breath pressure, easier of execution) and di maniera (requiring finer coordination with less breath force, more demanding); he notes these rarely coexist in one voice.
**Type:** Direct Observation
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Canto Fiorito

> "Henry Pleasants maintains that 'most, if not all, the devices of bel canto originated in an expressive purpose.'" (p. 169, citing Pleasants 1966, 21)

**Claim:** Pleasants argues that the ornamental devices of bel canto (roulades, trills, portamenti, appoggiaturas) were originally expressive in function, not merely decorative; Stark uses this to frame the entire chapter's argument.
**Type:** Interpretive Synthesis
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Bel Canto; Vocal Idiom

## The Trill (pp. 170–173)

> "Tosi and others considered the trill an essential ornament in professional singing... 'He who has a very beautiful trill, although deprived of every other ornament, always enjoys the advantage... And he who lacks it (or possesses a defective trill) will never be a great singer, even though he knows much.'" (pp. 170–171, citing Tosi 1986, 24–25)

**Claim:** Tosi makes the trill a sine qua non of professional singing, ranking it above all other ornaments; Stark sets this against later qualifications.
**Type:** Prescriptive Technique
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Trill

> "Two very strong obstacles are encountered in forming the trill perfectly. The first embarrasses the Master, for there has not yet been found an infallible rule for him who teaches it; the second confounds the Scholar, for ungrateful nature does not allow it to many, but to few." (p. 171, citing Tosi 1986, 24, 29)

**Claim:** Tosi acknowledges that no reliable pedagogical method for teaching the trill has been found, and attributes the ability primarily to natural endowment; this contradicts Garcia directly.
**Type:** Prescriptive Technique
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Trill

> "Garcia rejected the notion that the trill was a 'gift of nature,' and maintained that 'nothing is more erroneous than that opinion.' He urged students to 'seek the trill through the spontaneous trembling of the throat, and not through the progressive movement of the two notes.'" (p. 171, citing Garcia 1984, 163)

**Claim:** Garcia directly contradicts Tosi, asserting the trill can be taught; his method cultivates laryngeal oscillation rather than the deliberate alternation of two discrete pitches.
**Type:** Prescriptive Technique
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Trill

> "Francesco Lamperti... said: 'My opinion is, that the shake properly so called is a natural gift; and I am not convinced by the statements of Garcia and Duprez... Malibran and other well known singers possessed no natural shake, but acquired the executive shake.'" (p. 172, citing F. Lamperti 1884, 31)

**Claim:** F. Lamperti reinstates the natural-gift position against Garcia, while introducing the useful distinction between the "true" trill (laryngeal oscillation, innate) and the "executive" trill (learned rapid alternation); he cites Malibran as an example of the latter.
**Type:** Prescriptive Technique
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Trill

> "Francesco Lamperti argued that 'it is not indispensible. How often have I heard admirable artists, who touch the heart and evoke sympathy, without ever having recourse to the shake!'" (p. 173, citing F. Lamperti 1884, 31)

**Claim:** F. Lamperti explicitly qualifies Tosi's claim that the trill is indispensable, citing unnamed artists who moved audiences effectively without it; this is a direct counter-claim within the tradition.
**Type:** Prescriptive Technique
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Trill

## Tempo Rubato (pp. 173–176)

> "Tosi coined the term il rubamento di tempo (the stealing of time): 'He who does not know how to use rubato in singing... remains deprived of the best taste and the greatest intelligence.'" (p. 173, citing Tosi 1986, 99)

**Claim:** Stark credits Tosi (1723) with coining il rubamento di tempo; Hudson's research indicates this originally referred to melodic alteration against a steady accompaniment, not overall tempo change involving both voice and orchestra.
**Type:** Direct Observation
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Tempo Rubato

> "Garcia... 'The tempo rubato, on the contrary, accords this liberty only to the voice.'" (p. 174)

**Claim:** Garcia distinguishes tempo rubato (melodic liberty against a steady accompaniment) from accelerando/rallentando (overall tempo change); this is the clearest historical definition of rubato in the pedagogical literature Stark surveys.
**Type:** Prescriptive Technique
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Tempo Rubato

## Categories of Expressive Song and the Historical Arc (pp. 176–181)

> "Musicologist Manfred Bukofzer maintained that the musical style that emerged between 1630 and 1640... marked the beginning of bel canto... the 'exuberant coloratura' and 'garish virtuosity' of early opera were replaced by a lilting melodic flow, simple harmony, and more restrained vocal idiom." (pp. 177–178, citing Bukofzer 1947, 120–123)

**Claim:** Stark cites Bukofzer's definition of bel canto as a historically specific mid-17th-century style characterised by melodic restraint rather than virtuosic display; Stark judges this "a particularly narrow view" of what constitutes idiomatic and expressive singing.
**Type:** Interpretive Synthesis
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Bel Canto

> "After Garcia, authors such as Mathilda Marchesi, Julius Stockhausen, the Lampertis, and William Shakespeare restricted themselves largely to vocal techniques and did not relate those techniques to specific styles and composers." (p. 182)

## Modern Theoretical Grounding (pp. 180–183)

> "Sundberg... 'the human voice can be regarded as an organ which converts body language into sound. Perhaps this is important to music; it implies that it is the existence of the human voice which enables us [in] the highly sophisticated interpretation of sound which seems to be a prerequisite for musical communication.'" (pp. 181–182, citing Sundberg 1985a, 102)

**Claim:** Sundberg proposes that the singing voice converts bodily gestural patterns of emotional states into acoustic signals; Stark uses this to ground his argument that classical vocal idiom is a stylisation of bodily emotional gesture, not arbitrary convention.
**Type:** Empirical Finding
**Status:** V0
**AtlasNode:** Vocal Idiom

## Word-Note-Tone Case Study: "Che farò senza Euridice" (pp. 183–189)

> "One can readily hear characteristics of appoggio, with its 'held back' breath. The slight 'aftergrunts' heard at the end of phrases attest to the pent-up breath pressure, which, as suggested earlier, may evoke a feeling of pent-up emotion." (p. 187)

**Claim:** In Schipa's 1932 recording, Stark identifies audible evidence of appoggio (held-back breath, aftergrunts at phrase ends), covered singing on high notes, steady vibrato, legato, portamento, dynamic gradations including messa di voce, and slow tempo with effective use of accelerando; he presents this as a demonstration of classical vocal idiom carrying expressive weight that musical analysis alone cannot account for.
