# Reading Notes: Stark — Introduction

<!-- p. xvii -->
**The Problem of Definition:**
- **Bel canto** is a term "in search of a meaning."
- Literal translation: "beautiful singing."
- **Confusion:** No consensus on application. Ranges from "golden ages" to specific techniques to stylistic idioms.
- **Quote:** "Few musical terms are so loosely used or open to as many interpretations as 'bel canto'." — Jander (1980a).

**Historical Context:**
- Associated with the rise of the virtuoso singer (late 16th/early 17th c.).
- **Problem:** Early source material is "diffuse and imprecise." Reliance on oral tradition.
- **Sources:** Prefaces, chronicles, letters, treatises on ornamentation, travel diaries, scores (which are unreliable due to embellishment).
- **18th Century:** "Complete vocal method books" emerge but often lack specific instructions. Language is obscure.
- **Recordings:** Offer a remedy but can be manipulated (engineering).
- **Reviews:** Often reveal more about the critic than the singer.

<!-- p. xix -->
**Origin of the Term:**
- Did not come into general use until the **late 19th century**.
- Arisen from "pro- and anti-Wagner invective."
- **Philip Duey:** Reaction to Wagner's declamatory style caused Italians to rally around "bel canto" as a banner.
- **Nicola Vaccai (1840)** and **Francesco Lamperti (1864)** may have given the first impetus.
- **Owen Jander:** First references mid-19th c. when "weightier vocal tone" was prized over "light, florid delivery."
- **Vocal Weight:** Linked to larger orchestras, larger halls, and Romantic subject matter.

**Scholarly Definitions (Malleable Timeframes):**
- **Silva (1922):** Late 16th/early 17th c. florid ornamentation.
- **Goldschmidt:** Caccini's *Le nuove musiche* (1602).
- **Chrysander:** Zacconi's treatise (1592).
- **Haas & Bukofzer:** Cantabile style of Venetian/Roman operas (1630s–40s).
- **Della Corte:** Based on Tosi (1723) and Mancini (1777).
- **Harvard Dictionary (1969):** 18th c. opera/Mozart.
- **Harvard Dictionary (1986):** Mid-17th to early 19th c. ("golden age").
- **Celletti (1991):** Baroque (Monteverdi to Handel). Skips Mozart. Ends with Rossini. Excludes Romantic period.
- **Hermann Klein (1923):** Emphasises Mozart.
- **Jander (New Grove):** Extends to early 19th c.
- **Brand-Seltei (1972):** Extends to Manuel Garcia II and Russian national opera.

<!-- p. xx -->
**Critical Criteria (Also Malleable):**
- Based on treatises, composers, or style.
- **Castrati:** Duey/Foreman see them as the "most important element."
- **Blanche Marchesi:** Techniques useful for all styles, even Wagner.
- **Shaw's Critique:** Every teacher claims to have rediscovered Porpora's method; others are "quacks."
- **Pleasants:** Term appropriated by any teacher/singer satisfied they possess the "secret."
- **Limitation of Old Books:** Rich in imagery, poor in physiological/acoustic facts. Often fail to distinguish male/female or stylistic eras.

**Stark's Broad Definition:**
- **Bel canto** = The "classically trained voice" from Caccini to today.
- **Synonyms:** "Cultivated voice," "elite vocalism."
- **Requirements:** Refined use of laryngeal, respiratory, articulatory muscles.
- **Qualities:** Special timbre, evenness of scale/register, breath control, flexibility, tremulousness, expressiveness.
- **Contrast:** Differs from choral and "vernacular" (popular) singing.
- **Range:** From light/florid (Monteverdi–Rossini) to sustained/robust (Wagner).
- **Constant Theme:** Extraordinary use of the human voice.

<!-- p. xxi -->
**The "Simple" Argument (Critique):**
- **Duey:** "Amazingly direct and simple approach."
- **Foreman:** Precepts are simple but adequate.
- **Herbert-Caesari:** Foundation is the "completely natural voice" that needs no training.
- **Stark's Rebuttal:** If so simple, why is great singing "the art of the few"? Why did Herbert-Caesari write a book on technique?

**Stark's Strategy:**
- Examine physiological/acoustic properties of classically trained voices for qualities resembling historical descriptions.
- **Pre-Science:** Descriptions were vague/incorrect because the instrument is hidden.
- **1855 Turning Point:** **Manuel Garcia II** invents the **laryngoscope**.
- **Impact:** Verified theories; began the scientific approach.

<!-- p. xxii -->
**The Polarization (Science vs. Tradition):**
- **Detractors of Science:**
  - **G.B. Shaw:** Critical of Garcia's scientific approach.
  - **Franklyn Kelsey:** "Irony... if he himself had not invented the instrument which was to undermine our belief."
  - **Duey:** Science "broadened the field of controversy."
  - **Cornelius Reid:** Blamed "entry of the scientific investigator" for disappearance of techniques.
  - **Carl Seashore:** Musicians slow to accept scientific terminology.
- **Garcia's Legacy:** Polarised the field. Focus of disciples and detractors. Opened door to laryngologists.
- **Helmholtz (1862):** Paved way for modern vocal acoustics.
- **Garcia as Father:** Of modern voice science.

**Modern Voice Science:**
- **Focus:** Isolate specific physiological/acoustic aspects in lab.
- **Limitations:** Invasive instrumentation (catheters, electrodes), breath masks, short samples, rarely use world-class pros.
- **Growing Literature:** Useful studies on folds, pressure, airflow, resonance, vibrato, registers.
- **Goal:** Define parameters of good singing.

<!-- p. xxiii -->
**Outlook Differences:**
- **Traditional Teachers:** Retain old methods/terminology. Reject science as unnecessary/mischievous. Tools: demonstration, imitation, trial-and-error.
- **Medical Doctors:** Bias against "vigorous/robust" techniques (fear of abuse).
- **"No-Effort" School:** Early 20th c. doctors (US/UK/Germany) said high effort ruins voices; remedy = "relaxed" throat.
- **German Debate:** *Stauprinzip* (breath damming) vs. *Minimalluft* (minimum breath). Both claimed bel canto.

<!-- p. xxiv -->
**The Vibrato Debate:**
- **Historical:** Vague/equivocal. Good? Bad? Part of bel canto?
- **Early Music Specialists:** Embellishment added at points OR vocal fault to be eliminated.
- **Modern Scientists:** Normal/desirable feature. Integral part of training. Result of balanced muscular tension.
- **Stark's Stance:** Debate unresolved by history; will explore if idiomatic to classically trained voice.

**Idiomatic Singing:**
- **Crucial Concept:** Muscular balances manifest as specific vocal effects suited to trained voice.
- **Caccini:** Described good singing via *affetti* (emotions). Illustrated how voice quality, articulation, dynamics, ornaments heighten expression.
- **Garcia:** Expanded on this (breathing, timbres, registers, legato, portamento, *messa di voce*, trills, floridity).
- **Musicology Gap:** Ignore vocal idioms. Focus on "word-tone relationship" (words + notes).
- **Stark's Correction:** Expressive power lies in the **voice itself**. True relationship is **word-note-tone**.

<!-- p. xxv -->
**Stark's Conclusion/Definition:**
- **Defensible Concept:** Bel canto = specialised vocal techniques, idioms, expressive qualities of classically trained voice.
- **Not:** One stylistic era, single way of using voice, or specific set of conventions.
- **Is:** Irreducible vocal techniques adaptable to wide variety of styles/epochs without losing integrity.
- **Decline Myth:** "Decline of good singing" may be cyclical reaction to mutations, not loss of essentials.
- **Essential Components:** Still backbone of operatic singing.
- **Prism Analogy:** Bel canto = basic technique accommodating wide spectrum of styles.
- **Secrets:** Remain secret only because people ignore the authors who shared them.
- **Future:** Science will continue to reveal secrets as search enters 5th century.

**Claim:**

> [Stark, Introduction, p. xxv]

**Claim:** Bel canto is not a single historical era or style, but a set of irreducible vocal techniques (refined laryngeal/respiratory control, specific timbres, idiomatic expression) that can be adapted across historical epochs.
