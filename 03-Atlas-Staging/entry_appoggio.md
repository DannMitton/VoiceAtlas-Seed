## Note on aliases

**"Diaphragmatic breathing"** is commonly used as a synonym for *appoggio* in studio teaching. It is a useful metaphor that produces real results while being physiologically imprecise — Ian Howell's category of the "useful lie." *Appoggio* as classically defined involves antagonistic muscular coordination across the entire trunk, not diaphragmatic action alone. Voice Atlas flags it as a useful metaphor rather than a synonym.

---

## Summary

*Appoggio* — from the Italian *appoggiare*, to lean or to support — is the coordinated breath management system central to the Italian *bel canto* tradition. The term describes a dynamic muscular balance between inspiratory and expiratory forces that regulates subglottal pressure during phonation. It entered the print literature through the Lamperti school and has since been adopted, contested, and variously redefined by successive generations of pedagogues. Its meaning is not settled. The concept developed slowly and was fully formed only in the nineteenth century. It has become, in practice, a container into which different pedagogical traditions pour their preferred breath management strategies while claiming historical legitimacy — what Stark calls a "useful catch-word in vocal pedagogy." The prospect of a scientific consensus on proper breath control for singing is, in Stark's assessment, perhaps an illusory goal. *Appoggio* may be permanently better suited to the subjective insights of singers and voice teachers than to objective laboratory measurement.

### Claim 1 — The framing tension

The debate over breath control in singing is ancient and unresolved. Gasparo Pacchierotti (1740–1821) implied that a special manner of inhalation was the key to good singing. Dr Holder, cited by Charles Burney, maintained that it was the way breath was turned into tone that mattered most. These two positions frame a range of opinion that "seems as familiar today as it was then."

### Claim 2 — Lamperti's definition of singing *appoggiata*

"By singing *appoggiata*, is meant that all notes, from the lowest to the highest, are produced by a column of air over which the singer has perfect command, by holding back the breath, and not permitting more air than is absolutely necessary for the formation of the note to escape from the lungs."

### Claim 5 — Stark's two-part analytical definition

"The Italian word *appoggiare* means 'to lean.' I believe that in singing, this term has two specific applications. The first refers to the muscular antagonism between the inspiratory and expiratory breathing muscles during singing. This is often described by singers as a feeling of 'bearing down' with the diaphragm. The second refers to the role of the larynx in 'holding back,' or 'damming' the breath by means of glottal resistance, and by the intentional lowering of the larynx against the upward-bearing pressure of the breath."

### Claim 7 — *Appoggio* as catch-word

"Francesco Lamperti considered breath control to be the most important aspect of singing, and he used the concept of *appoggio* as a means of achieving that control. For him, *appoggio* was a broad term that referred not only to the balance of inspiratory and expiratory muscles, but also to vocal onset and glottal closure, position of the vocal tract, airflow and breath pressure, *legato*, the *messa di voce*, and even good intonation. Thus, *appoggio* became an inclusive term for breathing dynamics, and a useful catch-word in vocal pedagogy."

## Temporal evolution

**Pre-print oral tradition:** The concept existed in practice before it had a name. Tosi and Mancini describe breath management in terms of result and sensation rather than mechanism. The term *appoggio* does not appear in the earlier print literature by name.

**Early treatises (16th–17th century):** Maffei (1562), Zacconi (1592), Bovicelli (1594), and Caccini (1602) address breath economy pragmatically: breathe quietly, use the breath economically, do not breathe in the middle of passages. No theory of antagonistic muscular coordination yet.

**Eighteenth-century tutors:** Tosi, Mancini, and Martini address breath conservation and the *messa di voce* but do not articulate *appoggio* as a named concept. Martini's "holding back" the breath (1792) is the clearest precursor.

**Garcia school (mid-19th century):** Garcia devoted only half a page to respiration in his 1841 *Traité*, focusing on inhalation. For Garcia, tonal control derived primarily from correct glottal onset. Breath management was secondary. This created a lasting pedagogical divide.

**Mandl-Lamperti school (mid-to-late 19th century):** Mandl coins *lutte vocale* (1876). Francesco Lamperti embraces Mandl's framework and entrenches *appoggio* in the pedagogical literature. *Appoggio* becomes the central concept of the Italian school.

**Giovanni Battista Lamperti:** Extended his father's framework, emphasising elevated subglottal pressure and compressed breath. His *appoggio* required steady, elevated breath pressure on soft notes as well as loud ones.

***Stauprinzip* / *Minimalluft* debate (late 19th–early 20th century):** German reformulation of *appoggio* principles. *Stauprinzip* emphasises stronger breath damming for the powerful voices required by Wagner. *Minimalluft* opposes it, prescribing residual air and less breath pressure. Stark's conclusion: "The difference between *appoggio* and *Stauprinzip* is one of degree, not of kind" (p. 109).

**Miller synthesis (1977):** Expands *appoggio* to include resonance factors, positioning it as a total integrated system.

**Martienssen-Lohmann (1956):** Names *appoggio* as a *Ganzheit* — a total system. Stark concurs.

**Contemporary acoustic science:** The Edinburgh group (Leanderson and Sundberg 1988) establishes subglottal pressure as the decisive variable in phonation. Hixon and Weismer (1995) challenge their methods. Inter-singer variability among elite performers remains high. Donald G. Miller (1994) offers the brakes-and-accelerator analogy for the simultaneous action of inspiratory and expiratory muscles.
