Amendment accepted with one clarification needed.

## The issue

Your proposed display header "**Appoggio** (as defined by Stark 2003)" is wrong for *appoggio* specifically, and the reason matters for how we apply the rule going forward.

*Appoggio* is not Stark's proprietary term. It is a centuries-old Italian pedagogical concept traceable to the Lamperti school, Mandl, and the broader bel canto tradition. Stark is synthesising and interpreting that tradition, not coining a term. Labelling the entry "as defined by Stark 2003" would misrepresent the concept's provenance to every user who encounters it.

*Appoggio* is a universal concept currently sourced primarily from one text. That makes it `attribution_mode: provisional-consensus` — not `proprietary`. The Stark sourcing is transparent inside the entry, at the claim level, in the source record, and in the Kernels. That is sufficient. It does not belong in the display title.

## The corrected rule

**Proprietary concept** (Chapman's "Primal Sounds," Riggs's "Speech Level Singing"): The author coined the term or framework. Single source is correct and complete. Display title carries the author name: **Primal Sounds (Chapman)**.

**Universal concept, single source so far** (*appoggio*, *registration*, *chiaroscuro*): The concept belongs to the tradition, not to one author. The entry is `provisional-consensus`, kept in staging until a second source corroborates or contradicts. Display title is simply the concept name: **Appoggio**. Sourcing transparency lives inside the entry, not in the title.

## The test

Before assigning `attribution_mode`, ask: did this author coin this term, or are they one voice in an ongoing conversation about a shared term?

If coined: `proprietary`. Author name in display title.

If shared: `provisional-consensus` or `consensus` depending on source count. No author name in display title. Sourcing transparent inside the entry.

## Why this matters for users

A working teacher who sees "**Appoggio** (as defined by Stark 2003)" will reasonably conclude that Stark invented the concept. That is a misrepresentation Voice Atlas cannot afford. The display header is the first thing users read. It must be accurate.
