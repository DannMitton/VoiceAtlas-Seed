# Style Sheet — Daniel A. Mitton
*Living document. Rules are added as the work reveals them.*  
*Last updated: March 2026*

---

## Purpose

This style sheet governs all prose written under Daniel A. Mitton's name, including Voice Atlas entries, the article series *The Field Accounting for Itself*, correspondence, and any other scholarly or public writing. It exists to ensure consistency, to protect against the intrusion of AI-generated habits and patterns, and to keep the writing sounding like its author.

Collaborators and AI tools assisting with drafting must observe these rules without exception. When in doubt, read the prose aloud. If it sounds like a document and not a person, revise it.

---

## Core rules

| Rule | Principle | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Canadian spelling** | Use Canadian English throughout | Honour not honor. Recognise not recognize. Catalogue not catalog. |
| **Oxford comma** | Always use a serial comma before the final item in a list of three or more | Fields, Duey, Burgin, and Monahan. Not: Fields, Duey, Burgin and Monahan. |
| **No em-dashes** | Never use em-dashes. They are a dead giveaway of AI-assisted prose. Use a colon, a semicolon, parentheses, or restructure the sentence. | Not: The concept is contested — and has been for centuries. Use: The concept is contested; it has been for centuries. |
| **Asymmetrical sentence rhythm** | Vary length deliberately. A short sentence after a long one lands harder. Avoid sequences of sentences with identical structure or length. | Not: The field has a problem. The problem is attribution. Attribution matters. Instead: The field has an attribution problem. It always has. |
| **Jargon** | Use technical vocabulary only where it is necessary and economical. When a plain word serves, use it. When jargon names something precisely that plain language cannot, use it once, define it if needed, and trust the reader. | *Appoggio* names something plain language cannot. Breath support is adequate for general use. Choose deliberately. |
| **Foreign musical terms** | Italicise foreign musical terms that have not passed into general English usage. The test: would a literate non-musician recognise the word without glossing? If no, italicise. If yes, do not. | Italicise: *appoggio*, *bel canto*, *lutte vocale*, *messa di voce*, *passaggio*, *Stütze*, *coup de la glotte*. Do not italicise: tempo, piano, soprano, tenor, vibrato, aria. |

## Voice Atlas-specific rules

| Rule | Principle |
|---|---|
| **The Kernel** | One line, blunt, honest, in the founding author's voice. No hedging. No "some scholars argue." Say the thing. |
| **No pre-digesting** | Voice Atlas surfaces information and evidence. The reader connects the dots. Strategic Socratism is preferred over hand-holding. |
| **Claim language** | Be precise about what kind of claim is being made. "Stark argues," "Lamperti states," "Proctor maintains" — the verb matters. |
| **Edition specificity** | Always specify which edition is being cited. Never cite a work without an ISBN or OCLC anchor. |
